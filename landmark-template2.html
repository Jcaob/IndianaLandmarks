Senate Avenue YMCA

420 North Senate Avenue

Historical:Corner of Senate and Michigan Avenue (1912-1959)

Use Today:Parking lot

the Senate Avenue YMCA was one of the nation’s largest black YMCA and a beacon of hope in the struggle for equality.


As time passed, the Senate YMCA increasingly became a source of inspiration for tens of thousands of black people who continued to face discrimination and segregation in so many aspects of their daily lives. Under the leadership of Faburn E. DeFrantz, who served as executive secretary from 1916 to 1951, the Senate Avenue Y became the nation’s largest black YMCA and a beacon of hope in the struggle for equality.

Chapter 1

Monster Meetings

One of the organization’s most successful and noteworthy programs was its Sunday afternoon “Monster Meetings,” an ongoing lecture series that over the years brought to the podium national leaders such as Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., Thurgood Marshall, Paul Robeson, W. E. B. Du Bois, George Washington Carver, Langston Hughes, and Eleanor Roosevelt, as well as distinguished local figures. These speakers addressed the most critical social and political issues of the day. Although the Senate Avenue Y was demolished in 1959, the Monster Meeting tradition continues in a contemporary lecture series presented by the YMCA of Greater Indianapolis.
